This is for everyone – Accessibility

The web is fundamentally designed to work for humans, independent of their hardware, software, language, location, expertise or ability – enabling everyone to participate. Volunteers in the Wikimedia Movement have always been actively working and supporting inclusion and equal access.[1] Aligning to Wikimedia's vision[2] and to our principle “This is for everyone”, we aim for our interfaces to be accessible by design.

What and why accessibility?

Web accessibility measurements ensure that people with impairments or disabilities can perceive, understand, navigate, interact with, and contribute to the products we all create. As importantly, many measurements are improving general usability, resulting in easier-to-use interfaces that are Perceivable, Operable, Understandable and Robust for everyone.

These are the four principles of accessibility from the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines standard[3] (WCAG 2.0 onwards). They are informing the success criterias for achieved levels of conformance.

In the Wikimedia Design Style Guide and its resources we aim for level AA conformance.

Note, that our principles mainly focus on designing for:

  • Visual impairment needs
  • Motor impairment needs

Other categories like speech impairments don't apply in our graphical user-interface focus while hearing and cognitive impairments are to be covered on a content level, see for example Simple Wikipedia (English)[4] for being inclusive to users with learning difficulties.

Accessible by design

Colors and color palette – Use of color

Visual, motor

All colors of our palette feature level AA contrast ratio. We're following color-related accessibility rules:

  • Never use color as the only means of providing information or requesting an action.
  • The combinations of text and its background color must not fall below the WCAG recommended threshold ratio of 4.5:1 for standard text and 3:1 for larger text.

Fonts and typography – Legible and zoomable content

Visual

Choices of typeface, size, style, and spacing of text make it well readable in general, and are a must for some to be legible. Our choices advise on minimum font sizes. Those are also able to be overridden by user browser preferences – setting fonts in relative CSS units em or rem to provide text zooming when needed.

Touch and keyboard interaction – Ensure alternative input methods

Motor

When touch screens are the main interface, we must ensure minimum touch target sizes for happy human fingers. Additionally users with motor impairments profit from minimum target sizes for pointer inputs too.
All our components feature a strong outline for easily discoverable keyboard focus state.

Icons and images – Provide text alternatives

Visual

Icons, either with SVG title attribute or with text nodes visible only for screen readers when used in icon-only buttons.
Images should provide alt attribute with alternative text.
Both types must not include alternative text when solely used as decorative elements. title attributes are provided in WikimediaUI icon collection, and code in WikimediaUI theme of user-interface library OOUI.

Markup – Follow standards

Visual, motor

Markup for our components is provided in semantic HTML and follows Web Accessibility Initiative's ARIA Authoring practices standard[5, 6]. This lets assistive technology software identify them correctly. Additionally, it improves machine readability, resulting in better search optimization or alternative content delivery, for example in popular voice interfaces.
